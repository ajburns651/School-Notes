Act 1

Scene 1:

  • What effect is Shakespeare creating by beginning the play with this scene?
    • Shakespeare is creating the effect of conflict through the thunder and lightning, as well as good vs evil with the witches. He also sets the theme of the play through the words “Fair is Foul, and Foul is fair”. Creating suspense and mystery from the weird sisters.
  • What is the meaning of the line, “Fair is foul, and foul is fair”?
    • Fair is good looking on the in and out, and foul is bleh/gross. The meaning is that those who appear on the outside to be fair are actually on the inside foul(up to no good) while those who appear foul on the outside are fair. Things are not as they appear, and are in reality opposites.
  • What poetic device is used in this scene and to what effect?
    • Alliteration was used in this scene (F in fair foul fog and filthy), it allows the audience to consider and think about the meaning since the alliteration draws attention to it and emphasizes the witches base slovenliness.

Scene 2:

  • What is the purpose of this scene?
    • The purpose of this scene is that Duncan, who is the king of Scotland, hears of the victory of the war thanks to Macbeth and Banquo, and sends messengers to Macbeth to congratulate him and give him thane of Cawdor. We also learn of Macbeth as being a hero as he is brave
  • What image do readers have of Macbeth’s bravery and ability as a warrior?
    • We have the image that Macbeth is a hero who willingly risks his life as he charges into battle when the enemies swarm Macbeth and Banquo. That he is fearless, and a good man fighting for his country.
  • Who had Macbeth and Banquo been fighting?
    • Macbeth and Banquo were fighting soldiers of Norway and a rebellion led by Macdonwald.
  • What does King Duncan tell Ross to do?
    • King Duncan tells Ross to give Macbeth thane of Cawdor since the person who was originally the thane died in battle. Ross is basically king Duncan’s messenger as he is sent of to tell Macbeth of his new position/news

Scene 3:

  • What does the audience learn about the Weird Sisters from their conversation at the beginning of the scene?
    • The audience learns that the Weird Sisters are creating a storm because of someone that disrespected them. We get the impression that they are powerful and mischievous.
  • When Macbeth says, “So foul and fair a day I have not seen,” to what is he referring? What could be the dramatic irony in this line?
    • Macbeth is referring to the battle and the people that died along with him as foul, but their victory as fair. The dramatic irony is that he has not seen the foul and fair part of the day yet, as the witches telling him what he will be (thane of Cawdor, king) etc is actually a curse on his mentality as now he feels as he must do whatever he can to achieve it.
  • What prophecies do the Weird Sisters make regarding Macbeth? How does he react?
    • They make the prophecies that Macbeth will become Thane of Cawdor and King afterwards. He reacts by being intrigued while Banquo tells them to be quiet and he doesn’t care about what they say.
  • What do the Weird Sisters see in the future for Banquo?
    • The weird sisters see that the son’s of Banquo will become kings after Macbeth’s death. They will take over the thrown.
  • How do Macbeth and Banquo react to the experience after the Weird Sisters vanish?
    • Banquo thinks that they came and went and that it doesn’t matter what they say. They consider that the meds they took are causing delusions and corrupting their minds. Then they joke around with each other.
  • What “terms” does Ross indicate accompany the title Thane of Cawdor?
    • That he is both thane of cawdor and thane of glamis. He also states that the Thane of Cawdor is the most worthy thane, as it is thine.
  • As the others talk, what does Macbeth’s aside reveal about his thinking?
    • Macbeth’s aside reveals that he is curious and even thought about killing the king to fulfill his prophecy. He is starting to believe what the witches told him and he thinks it is too real and that it is his new destiny. Essentially becoming mentally crazy
  • How does Banquo’s comment support the “Fair is foul, foul is fair” theme?
    • Banquo’s comment supports the theme that he should be careful and not believe everything that he hears, which ultimately leads him to killing Duncan with the help of his manipulative wife. He is saying that while this all sounds great on the outside, something bad would likely come as a result of it.
  • What does Macbeth mean in his aside about two truths being prologue to the act of the imperial theme?
    • Macbeth means that both things that the witches said (thane of cawdor and king) will both come true, and that he will become the king (imperial theme). Macbeth in his aside means that these things sound good but there must be an ill that comes with them (cannot be ill, cannot be good)
  • What dramatic conventions does Shakespeare use to establish character and begin to lay out his tragedy?
    • Shakespeare uses a constant back and forth of Macbeth’s ideas/opinions to establish that he does not have the ruthlessness to kill king Duncan, and that he himself would decide not to kill Duncan. It lays out the tragedy of someone else convincing him to do so, as Banquo convinces him that his thoughts are ridiculous and that he is crazy and needs to rethink what happened as a single strange occurrence.

The porter’s soliloquy is in prose because it is supposed to show how he is from a lower class and is not as elegant as the rest. Lines 29-37 when he is responding to Macduff and talking about what alcohol causes one to do.

Act 3

Scene 1:

  • In his soliloquy, what suspicion and hope does Banquo reveal?
    • Banquo reveals that he is suspicious of Macbeths treason and murder of king Duncan. He hopes that Macbeth did not take the witches prophecy into his own hands and that his friend is still good.
  • Macbeth seems to be very interested in Banquo’s travel plans. Why do you suppose he is so interested?
    • Macbeth is very interested in Banquo’s travel plans because he wants to know where he is and at what time inorder for the two murders to murder him. It would be wise to do it away from Macbeth’s location so that he is not a suspect.
  • The speech beginning “To be thus is nothing…” is another one often quoted. What is the meaning of that sentence?
    • The meaning of that sentence is that to be King is nothing, but to be safely king is remarkable. This is because as a King, you almost always have a target on your back as someone wants to kill you.
  • What is there in Banquo’s character that makes Macbeth uneasy?
    • Macbeth is uneasy because he fears how Banquo is the good as he is honest and loyal, and fears that he will avenge the king and betray his friend.
  • What is there in the situation with Banquo that particularly upsets Macbeth?
    • Macbeth is upset that Banquo’s sons will be the air to the throne because he is a fruitless crown, meaning he has no offspring and therefore the royal crown will not be in his blood. Banquo’s sons basically benefit from no troubles at all.
  • What does Shakespeare offer in Macbeth’s conversation with the murderers?
    • Shakespeare on the surface offers that Macbeth would do it himself if he was not king, but he is really stating that Macbeth will not have the ability to kill his best friend himself because there is still some good in him.
  • How does Macbeth convince the murderers to kill Banquo and Fleance?
    • Macbeth manipulates the murders into thinking that Banquo is their enemy and has betrayed them. He makes them think that nothing should stop them from killing Banquo, which is why they are willing to kill Fleance.
  • What does Macbeth tell them to do?
    • Macbeth tells them to kill Banquo and Fleance tonight before supper while he is off and away riding his horse. He tells them to make sure the deed is done and to end it here once and for all.

    • The first apparition was an armed head, which is a medieval soldiers helmet but there was no face inside. The first apparition tells Macbeth to beware of Macduff, as we as readers know that Macduff will try and attack him for revenge. The second apparition is a bloody child, and tells him that he will never be killed by anyone born from a woman, which is everyone. The third apparition is a child crowned with a tree in his hand, which is significant because it tells Macbeth to be courageous and brave and that he will always be king until the woods reach the hill. The three apparitions are convincing Macbeth that he is invincible, making him arrogant and will eventually lead to his downfall.

    • Macbeth resolves that he will always be king and can watch the woods from the castle. He decides to prevent anyone from seizing the throne as he believes he can not be harmed.

    • Macbeth desires to know if Banquo’s kids will reign over this region, directly asking if Banquo’s kids will become future kings. He gets the answer that 8 of Banquo’s blood will become kings, the current sons of Banquo will have more sons and even they will become king.

    • This scene is significant because it convinces Macbeth that he is arrogant and can not be killed, and when he hears that Banquo’s kids will become king he realizes he needs to kill them all as he cannot stand hearing that.
  • What news does Lennox give to Macbeth?
    • Lennox tells Macbeth that Macduff has fled to England to take revenge against him.
  • What is Macbeth’s response?
    • Macbeth responds by stating that he is going to act on impulse. We all know this is a terrible idea for everyone as it results in illogical thinking.
  • What is his resolve at the end of this scene? How is this different from his previous actions?
    • Macbeth’s resolve is stating that he will murder Macduff and his family, and that he will kill anyone who is associated with Macduff. This is different from his previous actions in that he has only done what he has considered necessary, but now that he is arrogant he is going on a rampage, an endless cycle of violence. What also is different is that he has told someone else about his plans of murder.

Scene 2:

  • What does Shakespeare achieve with the conversation between Ross and Lady Macduff?
    • Shakespeare achieves the effect that Ross is attempting to tell Lady Macduff that Macduff left for a wise reason, but Lady Macduff only cares that Macduff left her and her son without protection and that he is a traitor to their family. It is written this way to signify the general society at that point in time stereotype of women.
  • What is the purpose of the exchange between Lady Macduff and her son?
    • The purpose of the exchange between Lady Macduff and her son reveals how her son understands why Macduff left and that his father is not dead or a traitor by leaving but doing an honorable and noble action. It also reveals how her son is more attached to Macduff than her mom, stating that he would find new parents if she did not weep for his death.
  • What effect is created by the Messenger’s entering to warn Lady Macduff?
    • The messenger’s pattern of speech creates the effect that danger is coming fast and that it will be so soon that he himself needs to leave them as fast as possible for his own safety. It also creates the effect of how Lady Macduff believes she won’t be killed because she did nothing wrong, which is not a convincing argument to mention to someone who is going to kill you.
  • Why does Shakespeare show the son’s murder onstage? Why is Lady Macduff’s murder offstage?
    • Shakespere shows the son’s murder onstage and Lady Macduff’s murder offstage because of the public opnion of gender rolls at that time. It is considered incorrect to murder a women on stage, but it is interesting in that Shakespeare murders women onstage in his other plays.

Scene 3:

  • What additional evidence does Shakespeare give his audience that Macbeth is a tyrant?
    • Shakespeare also gives the evidence of Macbeth being a tyrant through his comparison to Lucifer, and how even though he was a brave and honest man he still fell to evil.
  • What suspicion of Macduff does Malcolm voice?
    • Malcolm voices the suspicion that Macduff is on Macbeth’s side and is tasked to murder him, and Macduff argues that he is not and that he is not treacherous. Malcolm needed to make sure of Macduff’s intentions and position before stating any more.
  • How do Malcolm’s comments about Macbeth again bring to mind the “fair is foul” theme?
    • Malcolm’s comment that “though all things foul would wear the brows of grace parallels the theme of fair is foul and foul is fair because Malcom agrees that those who are foul on the inside do not appear on the inside, relating to his earlier suspicion of Macduff actually being on Macbeth’s side and that he is his servant.
  • What literary device is Shakespeare employing when Malcolm says, “Angels are bright still, though the brightest fell”?
    • Shakespeare employs an allusion as he alludes to Lucifer, who is a fallen angel who is unhappy with his life in hell.
  • What is it that makes Malcolm suspicious of Macduff’s motives?
    • Malcolm is suspicious of Macduff’s motives because of him leaving his wife and child unprotected which could signal that they are under Macbeth’s protection. In reality, Macduff leaving his wife and child unprotected actually led to their slaughter by the murderers.
  • How does Malcolm test Macduff’s honesty?
    • Malcolm tests Macduf’s honesty by stating what terrible things he would do as a king, in hope that Macduff’s loyalty to the good of his country would reveal his honesty, and therefore his hatred towards Macbeth. Malcolm states concerning things such as needing prostitutes and destroying society for wealth.
  • How does Macduff nearly fail the test?
    • Macduff nearly fails the test when he agrees with Malcom that Malcom would make a great king and that there would be many out there to fill his lust. Macduff is kind of Malcoms yes man here in that he does not challenge him in any way.
  • What finally convinces Malcolm?
    • Macduff stating that they are doomed if Macbeth and Malcolm are in control of Scotland helps convince Malcolm that Macduff is on his side. Macduff states that things have all gone downhill since the murder of Duncan, further revealing his position of Macbeth not being a good leader.
  • What purpose does the brief dialogue about Edward the Confessor’s alleged ability to heal serve?
    • It serves the idea that not all kings are bad and how great queen elizabeth’s father was. This is because Shakespeare would present his plays to the queen, and that he wanted to make sure that she liked it by stating that not all kings are tyrants.
  • After receiving word of his family’s slaughter, what does Macduff resolve?
    • Macduff decides to take revenge against Macbeth and with the help of troops and others, overthrow Scotland and retake control of their once sacred country. When they exit, they are off to takedown macbeth.

Act 5

Scene 1:

  • Explain how this entire scene is ironic.
    • The entire scene is ironic because the doctor is unable to help her with her own mental issues and Lady Macbeth is just washing her hands that are clean. It is also ironic in that Lady Macbeth reveals the murders that Macbeth committed to her gentlewoman and doctor even though she was the one since the beginning that said to not tell anyone about the crimes they have committed.
  • What is ironic about Lady Macbeth’s constant “handwashing”?
    • Lady Macbeth is repeatedly attempting to wash her sings and blood on her hands (guilt) away, but that is not possible because there are no real spots on her hands, just the mental guilt that she is stuck with. There is also a callback to no amount of water can wash the guilt away.
  • What does the Doctor’s “Foul whisperings are abroad” speech echo?
    • The Doctor’s “Foul whisperings are abroad” speech echoes the saying that unnatural things lead to troubles, which is essentially the foundation of guilt. His speech also reveals how for one to relieve themself from guilt they need to do so themselves, and no person or doctor can do that for them, which is why therapists are there for you to talk about yourself and vent, not for them to tell you how you should feel.

Scene 2-7:

  • What effect is Shakespeare creating with this sequence of short scenes which follow one another so closely?
    • Shakespeare creates the effect that all of these events are occurring at the same time. If Shakespeare divided the scenes into larger ones, then we would miss the reactions Macbeth has and the story telling of the woods reaching dunsinane hill.
  • Whose side are Lennox, Angus, Menteith, and Caithness on?
    • Lennox, Angus, Menteith, and Caithness are on Malcolm’s side, and are against Macbeth. They are on their way to meet Malcom and the other English soldiers in the woods.
  • What do they say about the men that Macbeth commands?
    • They say that the men who listen to Macbeth’s commands do so because of his position of power, not his love for them or how great of a king he is. This is a similar idea to most other tyrants, whose men are just trying to avoid being killed.
  • What is Macbeth’s lament in his “my way of life Is fallen into the sear …” speech?
    • Macbeth’s lament in his speech is that he is no longer an honest, brave, or great man like he once was, and therefore he does not deserve to live. He also states that the only honor he would have is from people saying that he is honorable, but he realizes deep down that it is all a lie, and why live a lie. He then states that he doesn’t have love, obedience, or troops of friends… because he killed them all. He is reflecting on how his life has turned out compared to how it was supposed to be.
  • What is Lady Macbeth’s condition, and how does Macbeth believe it could be cured?
    • Lady Macbeth’s condition is a mental condition and can not be cured by a doctor, she is not sick in the way that is normally thought of, which was probably thought of as rare at that time. Macbeth believes that the Doctor can easily cure it by giving her medicine and remove the troubles in her brain from her memory.
  • What tactical strategy does Malcolm take?
    • Malcolm decides to task the soldiers with cutting down parts of trees to shield their appearance and sheer number of soldiers from Macbeth as they march up the hill. This is filling the witches prophecy as the woods are making their ware to Dusaine hill.
  • In reaction to the news that Lady Macbeth is dead, Macbeth delivers his most famous soliloquy. What does it mean?
    • Macbeth’s most famous soliloquy reveals his outlook of life in that everyday people slowly creep through life just to die. Everyone ends up in the same place and nobody remembers you once you’re dead. He compares this to a “player” or actor on stage, in that when they are on stage they are in the spotlight but as soon as they leave they are neglected and no one cares who they are. “Signifying nothing” in the end, “heard no more”
  • What does Macbeth recognize and how does it fit in with the “fair is foul” theme?
    • Macbeth recognizes that something that sounded good to him turned out to be evil. (His interpretation of the woods never being able to reach the hill thought that it meant he would never be defeated). This fits into the fair is foul and foul is fair theme because when he states “That lies the truth” he agrees with the theme of the book because the truth can be changed to deceive others, ex white lies.
  • As bad as things look for Macbeth, why does he still scorn all his opponents?
    • He still scorns all of his opponents because he believes that he can not be killed by anyone that is born from a woman, which he decides is everyone. So he is still overconfident and marches straight into battle thinking that he can not be killed.
  • What is the meaning of Siward’s statement that Macbeth’s men “on both sides do fight”?
    • Siward’s statement means that the country is torn apart in that Macbeth’s men are fighting because they are tasked with that but they really want to fight against Macbeth due to how evil he is. Some soldiers are rebelling against him.

Scene 8:

  • What information does Macduff tell Macbeth that makes him frightened (“Cow’d my better part”)?
    • Macduff tells Macbeth that he was not born from a woman and was born with what is known now as a C-section. This frightens Macbeth because the prophecy only said that he could not be killed by anyone born by a woman, but since Macduff is not he is scared.
  • What is Macbeth’s response?
    • Macbeth becomes a coward and tries to run away and not fight, as he realizes that he might die here and that Banquo’s sons will become king
  • Why does he quickly change his mind about fighting?
    • He does not want to live in a world where Malcolm is king and decides that he would rather flee or die trying to prevent that. He realizes the original prophecy is becoming true and can not live with it.
  • On what note does the play end?
    • The play ends on the fact that Malcolm is crowned king and Scotland is returning to normal. Also instead of thanes, people are earls, due to Shakespeare and Queen Elizabeth’s connection.
