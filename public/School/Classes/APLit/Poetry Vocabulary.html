Poetry Vocabulary

Elements

  • Stanza: a group of consecutive lines in a poem that form a single unit
  • Meter: a regular pattern of stressed and unstressed syllables
    • ˘ / ˘ / ˘ / ˘ / ˘ / ˘ / ˘ / ˘ /
    • I do not like green eggs and ham, I do not like them Sam I am
  • Iambic pentameter: a line of poetry containing five beats: unstressed syllable then a stressed syllable
    • ˘ / ˘ / ˘ / ˘ / ˘ /
    • But SOFT, what LIGHT from YONder WINdow BREAKS?
  • Rhyme: the repetition of accented vowel sounds following them in words that are close together in a poem (such as mean and screen). Not all poems rhyme.
  • End Rhyme: rhymes at the end of lines of poetry
    • The world would end tomorrow afternoon
    • But short of that, it might be too soon
  • Rhyme Scheme: the pattern of end rhymes in a poem
    • Little Miss Muffet a
    • Sat on a tuffet a
    • Eating her curds and whey b
    • Along came a spider c
    • And sat down beside her c
    • And frightened Miss Muffet away b
  • Internal Rhyme: rhymes within lines of poetry
    • Back in the chamber turning, all my soul within me burning.
  • Approximate/Slant Rhyme: rhymes involving sounds that are similar but not exactly the same
    • (such as leave/live)
  • Refrain: a repeated sound, word, phrase, line, or group of lines
    • “I gotta a feeling, that tonight’s gonna be a good night, that tonight’s gonna be a good night”
  • Quatrain: four lined stanzas of any kind—rhymed, metered, etc.
  • Prose: any writing that is not poetry
    • (essays, novels, short stories, etc.)
  • Couplet: two consecutive lines in a poem, usually rhyming
    • The outlook wasn’t brilliant for the Mudville nine that day;
    • The score stood four to two, with but one inning more to play;
  • Onomatopoeia: the use of words whose sounds imitate or suggest their meaning
    • (buzz, rustle, boom, tick tock, crunch, sizzle)
  • Speaker: the voice talking to us in the poem
    • The voice is sometimes, but not always, the poet. Think of the __ (or the voice) as a character the author has created.
  • Pun: a play on words, either on different senses of the same word or on the similar sense or sound of different words
  • Assonance: repeated vowel sounds in non-rhyming words
    • He was way late to pick the grapes in May.
  • Alliteration: the repetition of the same consonant sounds in words that are close together, or the repetition of consonant sounds that are very similar
    • She stopped and stood still for a long time

Types

  • Ode: a lyric poem, rhymed or unrhymed, that praises something or someone
  • Narrative: a poem that tells a story
  • Free Verse: a poem that has no pattern of meter or regular rhyme scheme
  • Lyric Poem: a poem that expresses the feelings or thoughts of a speaker; It does NOT tell a story
  • Ballad: a song or songlike poem that tells a story mostly dealing with adventure or romance
  • Elegy: a poem of mourning or grief, usually about someone who has died
  • Sonet: a fourteen-line poem, usually written in iambic pentameter. Shakespeare wrote these with a specific rhyming scheme of:
    • ABAB CDCD EFEF GG
  • Lymeric: a very short humorous or nonsensical poem with five lines and an AABBA rhyme scheme
